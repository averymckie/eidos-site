eidos

Eidos roughly means the shape of how something is organized and how it functions. For technology programs with multiple vendors, compliance requirements, and operational teams, that means defining what readiness actually looks like and getting the teams involved aligned on it before go-live.

Technology Readiness | Delivery Coordination | Regulated Environments

Most complex technology programs have competent individual teams. The failures happen between those teams at the handoffs, the integration points, and the places where one group's assumptions about another group's commitments went unconfirmed. My job is to find those gaps, define what each team actually needs from the others, and write the documents the teams work from. I have done this for eight years across highly regulated environments where getting it wrong carried compliance, safety, and operational consequences.

Problems I Solve

Production Readiness

Programs reach their go-live date and the question of whether the functions are actually prepared has been treated as something that will resolve on its own. My job is to make sure that answer is documented and agreed on well before the milestone, using go/no-go criteria, acceptance standards, and evidence requirements that are in place before a readiness review opens.

Vendor Integration

Most times, when multiple vendors deliver into the same environment on independent timelines, integration conflicts between their systems stay hidden unless someone is tracking them against a shared baseline. My job is to synchronize vendor readiness timelines and sequence milestone confirmations so those conflicts show up where they can be resolved instead of in production.

Operations Handoff

The teams who build systems and the teams who support them in production often work from different assumptions about how monitoring, alerting, escalation, and who owns what should function. Closing that gap means working with both sides to write the definitions, process documentation, and support requirements that make the transition to day-to-day operations unambiguous for the people involved.

Compliance Alignment

In regulated environments, requirements need to preserve regulatory intent through the translation into technical specifications. That translation passes through multiple teams and multiple levels of abstraction. Most times, when this happens, regulatory meaning gets diluted at these steps unless someone is specifically protecting it. I have found this to be true across federally regulated infrastructure, healthcare, and financial services.

Dependency Resolution

Interdependent teams frequently operate on assumptions about what other teams have committed to without having confirmed those commitments. My job is to work with these teams to surface those gaps, figure out who owns what, document what the teams have actually agreed to do, and confirm what needs to happen before the next step can move.
