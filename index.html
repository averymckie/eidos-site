The Core Insight

When the logic of a process is written down completely, automation succeeds. When the logic exists only in people's heads, automation fails. This pattern holds across every case we've examined.

About 70% of large-scale automation projects miss their targets. This number has remained consistent across decades, technologies, and industries. The technology keeps changing; the failure rate does not. The question is why.

The answer turns out to be structural. The characteristics that determine automation success are identifiable before implementation begins. They have been consistent across a century of automation history, from Ford's assembly line in 1913 to modern AI deployments. The technology changes; the patterns do not.

Most organizations learn whether their processes can automate during implementation, when it becomes expensive. We do this analysis first, telling you what will work before you commit the resources.

What We Do

The Assessment

We analyze the structure of your processes and tell you what will automate before you invest. The analysis examines how the work actually operates, where the decision points are, and whether the underlying logic has been specified at the level required for automation.

The result is a clear answer: proceed as planned, restructure the process first, or do not automate this work. With the structural evidence behind the recommendation, not just consultant opinion.

This approach has been validated against 81 historical automation cases spanning from 1913 to 2024, across 20 industries. We have also analyzed over 14,000 professional tasks across 175 domains. The patterns that determine success are consistent. No exceptions have been found.
