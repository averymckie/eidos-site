Approach

Orientation

The first step is reading the existing materials, including project plans, architecture documents, governance materials, vendor contracts, and process documentation, and then talking to the people doing the work. The documentation describes what should be happening while the people describe what is actually happening. Identifying where those two pictures disagree reveals the real problems. These problems are usually common knowledge among the people closest to the work but undocumented at the level where decisions get made.

Specification

The result of those conversations is a set of documents that define readiness for the engagement. This includes ownership and support definitions, go-live criteria, process documentation, and monitoring requirements, written for the specific environment, the specific vendors, and the specific compliance constraints involved. The purpose remains consistent: give the team that inherits the system what they need to do their job without filling in blanks with their own assumptions. Most times, when documents are vague, the teams fill those blanks differently, and the assumptions diverge at the boundaries.

Coordination

The documents work when the teams acting on them understand and agree to them. Achieving that agreement means holding working sessions, tracking dependencies, sequencing milestones, and resolving the conflicts that surface when independent teams align their assumptions against a shared baseline. I have found that a significant portion of the difficulty in complex programs lives in this alignment work. The individual contributions are usually competent, but the breakdowns happen where functions meet. Those seams become visible when someone is tracking them at once.

Handoff

The materials written during an engagement are built to outlast the engagement. The team that inherits the system gets the documents they need to run it, including monitoring requirements, clear definitions of who owns what, governance rules, and step-by-step procedures. The goal is lasting clarity because the people who operate a system after go-live need to know what to do whether the person who wrote it down is still in the room or not.
