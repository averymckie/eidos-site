About Eidos

Pattern Recognition, Not Intuition

This isn't consulting intuition. It's pattern recognition backed by information theory, developed from observing structural failures across 20+ organizations.

The Founder

Avery McKie

Over two decades of enterprise IT transformation work across financial services, utilities, healthcare, defense, and technology sectors revealed a consistent pattern: automation projects fail for structural reasons that are identifiable before implementation begins.

The methodology didn't emerge from academic theory. It emerged from observing the same failure patterns repeat across 20+ organizations, regardless of industry, vendor, or technology stack.

After documenting these patterns and mapping them to information theory principles (Shannon, Turing, Wiener), the structural assessment methodology became a systematic way to predict automation outcomes before capital is committed.

The Core Insight

"Automation succeeds when process logic is explicit. It fails when process logic is tacit."

This distinction, visible in every failure case study, provides the diagnostic lens for structural assessment. If the logic lives in documentation, it can be automated. If it lives in someone's head, automation will fail.

The Methodology

How the Patterns Became a System

From observation to formalization: the evolution of structural assessment.

1

Pattern Recognition

Years of transformation work revealed that automation failures weren't random. They clustered around specific structural characteristics.

The same warning signs appeared whether the project was ERP implementation, workflow automation, or AI deployment.

2

Theoretical Grounding

Mapping observed patterns to information theory provided the explanatory framework: Shannon (signal vs. noise), Turing (computability), Wiener (feedback loops).

What looked like "implementation problems" were actually information structure problems, identifiable in advance.

3

Systematic Assessment

The structural assessment methodology formalizes these patterns into a repeatable diagnostic process: decompose, classify, quantify, recommend.

Now organizations can identify automation risk before committing capital, not after discovering it during implementation.

Our Name

Why "Eidos"

Eidos (pronounced eye·dos) comes from the Greek word εἶδος, meaning "form," "essence," or "the underlying structure that gives something its character."

Structural assessment is about seeing the essential form of a process, distinguishing what's explicitly documented from what exists only in human minds. The name reflects the work: revealing the true structure beneath surface appearances.
