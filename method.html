The Eidos Method

Information Theory Applied to Process Analysis

A rigorous framework for predicting automation outcomes, grounded in the foundational science of computation and communication.

The Theoretical Foundation

Our method draws on three giants of 20th-century science who defined the fundamental limits of what machines can and cannot do:

S

Claude Shannon

Information theory and the mathematical limits of communication. His work reveals when information can be transmitted without loss—and when it cannot.

T

Alan Turing

Computability theory and the limits of algorithmic processes. His work defines what can be computed—and what remains forever beyond machine capability.

W

Norbert Wiener

Cybernetics and the science of control systems. His work explains when feedback loops stabilize—and when they spiral into chaos.

The Core Principle

Information that exists only in human minds cannot be automated. Information that is fully explicit and codified can be.

"Automation succeeds when process logic is explicit. It fails when process logic is tacit."

The Key Distinction

Explicit vs. Tacit Process Logic

Every business process contains decision points. The nature of those decisions determines automation potential.

Explicit Logic

Rules that can be written down completely. If-then statements that cover all cases. Decision trees with defined endpoints.

"If order > $10,000, require manager approval"
"If payment received, update status to 'Paid'"
"If inventory < reorder point, generate PO"

Automation potential: High

These processes can be fully automated with predictable results.

Tacit Logic

Judgment calls that "you just know." Expertise that can't be articulated. Context-dependent decisions based on experience.

"Use your judgment on this exception"
"Senior staff handle the complicated cases"
"You'll learn to recognize these over time"

Automation potential: Low to None

Attempting to automate these processes leads to failure.
