Why Automation Fails

The Consistent Pattern

The failure pattern is consistent: projects attempt to automate processes whose underlying logic has never been fully specified. The implementation forces the organization to confront decisions that were previously made by experienced people exercising judgment. Budget and timeline overruns accumulate as those undocumented decisions surface and must be resolved.

This is not a new observation. The mathematical principles were established between 1936 and 1948 by Shannon, Turing, and Wiener. What they demonstrated is that there are structural limits to what can be formalized and processed by machines. These limits are not technological; they are mathematical. No amount of better software changes them.

What determines automation success is whether the work has the structural characteristics that support it. Those characteristics are identifiable before you invest.

What We Assess

The Structure of Work

We examine how the work actually operates: where decisions are made, what information flows between steps, what happens when things deviate from the normal path.

The assessment answers specific questions. Which parts of this process have logic that is fully specified? Which parts depend on judgment that has never been documented? Where would automation break because it encounters decisions that require human knowledge to resolve?

The output is not a theoretical feasibility study. It is a structural map showing exactly which parts of the process can automate, which parts need modification, and which parts should remain human-operated. With the evidence behind each determination.

The Difference

What Makes This Different

Most technology assessments ask whether a given software can handle a given process. This is the wrong question. The right question is whether the process itself is automatable, independent of what technology you use.

A process that depends on undocumented judgment calls will fail regardless of what software you implement. A process with fully documented logic will likely succeed with any competent implementation. We answer this prior question, telling you whether the conditions for success exist before you evaluate vendors.

The Validation

Tested Against History

The approach has been tested against 81 historical automation cases spanning from 1913 to 2024. The cases include well-documented failures like Phoenix, Knight Capital, and Hertz/Accenture, as well as dozens of less prominent cases across 20 industries. We have also analyzed over 14,000 professional tasks across 175 domains.

The patterns that determine success are consistent across every case examined. This is not a claim that the method is perfect. It is a claim that no one has found a case where it fails.

The Foundation

Built on Public Science

The underlying principles are not proprietary. Shannon's information theory is public. Turing's work on computability is public. Wiener's cybernetics is public. What we provide is the application of these principles to the specific question of automation outcomes.

This means the analysis is auditable. We are not asking you to trust a black box. The mathematical foundations you can verify independently. The question is whether they apply to your situation, and our assessment shows you how.
